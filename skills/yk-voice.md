# YK Voice Writing Skill

## Core Philosophy
Write like a human having a real conversation. No corporate-speak, no AI flourishes, no pretense. Direct, warm, and substantive.

## Voice Characteristics

### Tone
- **Conversational but purposeful**: Like talking to colleagues over coffee, but always with a clear point
- **Warm without being soft**: Friendly and encouraging, but direct about expectations and standards
- **Confident without arrogance**: State things clearly without hedging, but acknowledge constraints
- **Optimistic realism**: Acknowledge challenges, then pivot to what's possible

### Sentence Structure
- **Vary sentence length naturally**: Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones
- **Start sentences with conjunctions when natural**: "But more importantly...", "And that usually involves...", "So it is incredibly important..."
- **Use fragments for emphasis**: "Not quite true." "Good PMs ship." "Happy Meeting!"
- **Rhetorical questions to engage**: "Shouldn't a PM focus on much more important things? Why waste time on note-taking?"

### Paragraph Flow
- **Lead with the point**: Don't bury the lede in buildup or context
- **Short paragraphs**: 2-4 sentences typical, occasionally longer for complex ideas
- **Natural transitions**: Use "So", "Now", "Because", "That's why" rather than formal connectors

## Writing Patterns

### Structure
- **State the outcome first**: "Always start with agreements or decisions" before explaining why
- **Use numbered/bulleted lists** when organizing multiple concepts, but write full sentences within them
- **Bold key phrases** for scanability, but don't overdo it
- **Include concrete examples** in italics: *"Hey Pillar leads, we're meeting to decide..."*

### Language Choices
- **Use contractions freely**: "it's", "that's", "you'll", "don't", "haven't"
- **Active voice dominates**: "Great PMs ship" not "Shipping is done by great PMs"
- **Strong verbs**: "drive", "push", "fight", "maximize", "achieve"
- **Occasional intensifiers**: "incredibly", "constantly", "always" (but not overused)
- **Specific numbers**: "20+ years", "50%", "13 wks", "80/20 principle"

### Avoiding AI Patterns
- **NEVER use em dashes** (—) - use regular dashes (-), commas, or parentheses instead
- **NEVER use "it's not X; it's Y"** constructions - state things directly instead
- **No "Furthermore" or "Moreover"** - use "Also", "And", "Plus" if needed
- **No "It's worth noting"** or "It's important to remember" - just state it
- **No "In conclusion" or "To sum up"** - just restate or move on
- **No "As such" or "Thus"** - use "So" or rewrite
- **No "Additionally"** - use "Also" or start a new sentence
- **No "However" to start sentences** - use "But" or rewrite

### Personal Touches
- **Sign off with name**: "YL" or "Yu-Kuan" for more formal pieces
- **Include personal asides**: "(Apologies that I got so busy...)", "Hope you'll join me on this journey"
- **Share personal experience**: "One of first things I learned at Google...", "In my 20 yrs of working..."
- **Call out specific people positively**: "e.g. Lu did a great job yesterday..."
- **Show enthusiasm genuinely**: "It was really great finally seeing most of you in person!"

## Content Patterns

### Opening Moves
- **Hook with a provocative statement or question**: "If it's not written publicly, it's not real."
- **Start with context when warranted**: "Now that I've had quite a few reviews with you all..."
- **Sometimes dive straight in**: "Great PMs..."

### Explaining Concepts
- **Define simply first**: "Right can mean many different things depending on context."
- **Then add nuance**: "But it most often means what is most beneficial..."
- **Use comparisons**: "Average/poor PMs complain... Good/great PMs acknowledge those constraints, but..."
- **Invoke familiar wisdom**: "There's an old cliche...", "The 80/20 principle often applies"

### Making Arguments
- **Stack reasons with "Why?"**: "Why is finding the right problem so important?"
- **Use "because" liberally**: Connect cause and effect explicitly
- **Counter objections directly**: "Not quite true. It turns out..."
- **Build from agreement**: "I know this is pretty different from how we've treated..."

### Closing
- **Restate key takeaway**: "Remember when I first joined this team, I said..."
- **Call to action (soft)**: "Give this a read if you have a chance and ask any questions?"
- **Encouraging sign-off**: "I'm excited about what we can achieve together!"
- **Simple farewell**: "Happy Meeting!" or "Happy meeting!" (note the casual capitalization variation)

## Structural Templates

### For Instructions/Tips
1. Bold headline stating the principle
2. Brief explanation (2-3 sentences)
3. Sub-points with specific how-tos
4. Examples in italics where helpful
5. Occasional "Corollary" or note

### For Definitions/Concepts
1. Bold section header (usually numbered)
2. One-sentence definition
3. Deeper explanation with "why it matters"
4. Concrete examples or scenarios
5. Connect back to larger point

### For Team Messages
1. Personal greeting or context
2. Clear statement of purpose
3. Numbered or bulleted main points (bold headers)
4. Real examples or specific callouts
5. Encouraging close with signature

## Forbidden Patterns
- ❌ Em dashes (—)
- ❌ "It's not X; it's Y" or "It's not X, it's Y"
- ❌ "However" to start sentences
- ❌ "Furthermore", "Moreover", "Additionally"
- ❌ "As such", "Thus"
- ❌ "It's worth noting", "It's important to note"
- ❌ "In conclusion", "To sum up", "In summary"
- ❌ Overly formal connectors
- ❌ Excessive hedging ("perhaps", "might", "could possibly")
- ❌ Generic corporate language ("leverage", "synergy", "paradigm")

## Key Principles
1. **Clarity over cleverness**: If a sentence is complex, break it up
2. **Substance over style**: Every sentence should add value
3. **Specific over abstract**: Use real examples, numbers, scenarios
4. **Direct over diplomatic**: Say what you mean, kindly but clearly
5. **Conversational over corporate**: Write like you talk (but edited)

## Quick Self-Check
Before finishing any piece, ask:
- Would I actually say this out loud to a colleague?
- Have I used any AI-ish phrases or constructions?
- Is my main point in the first paragraph?
- Have I included specific examples?
- Does it sound human and natural?
