# Arc Templates Reference

Detailed scene-by-scene templates for each supported narrative arc. Each template includes time splits, scene purposes, voiceover guidance, and visual direction patterns.

---

## Arc 1: The Transformation Arc (Default)

**Structure**: Problem → Shift → Workflow/Demo → Results
**Best for**: Product demos, corporate vision, AI transformation, capability showcases
**Emotional shape**: Frustration → Hope → Momentum → Conviction

### Time Splits by Duration

| Duration | Scene 1 (Problem) | Scene 2 (Shift) | Scene 3 (Workflow) | Scene 4 (Results) |
|----------|-------------------|-----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| 60 sec   | 0:00–0:12 | 0:12–0:15 | 0:15–0:42 | 0:42–0:60 |
| 90 sec   | 0:00–0:18 | 0:18–0:22 | 0:22–0:65 | 0:65–0:90 |
| 120 sec  | 0:00–0:18 | 0:18–0:23 | 0:23–0:95 | 0:95–1:20 |
| 180 sec  | 0:00–0:25 | 0:25–0:32 | 0:32–1:35 | 1:35–1:80 |

### Scene Descriptions

**Scene 1 — THE PROBLEM (15–17% of total)**
- Purpose: Establish the pain the audience recognizes
- VO tone: Observational, empathetic, slightly tense
- Visuals: Stock footage of the current state — overwhelmed professionals, cluttered screens, manual processes
- Music: Subtle, reflective, measured pace
- Key rule: Don't exaggerate. Sophisticated audiences respect understatement. Describe reality; don't catastrophize.
- Ends with: The cost of the status quo (time, money, missed opportunity)

**Scene 2 — THE SHIFT (3–5% of total)**
- Purpose: Pivot moment — signal that a better way exists
- VO tone: Confident, simple, declarative
- Visuals: Clean transition. Product UI or brand moment. Design system colors take over.
- Music: Gentle upward shift
- Key rule: One sentence. Maybe two. This is a breath, not a scene.

**Scene 3 — THE WORKFLOW / DEMO (55–60% of total)**
- Purpose: Show the solution in action with specificity and momentum
- VO tone: Narrative, building energy, descriptive not promotional
- Visuals: Product footage, UI interactions, data visualizations, split-screens showing input/output
- Music: Modern, clean electronic — building steadily
- Key rule: Break into 2–4 beats. Each beat is a distinct moment (e.g., morning/afternoon, step 1/step 2, customer A/customer B). Label each beat.
- Data overlays: Show real metrics, activation rates, time savings — not adjectives

**Scene 4 — THE RESULTS / TRANSFORMATION (20–25% of total)**
- Purpose: Land the impact. Give the audience the number or the feeling that sticks.
- VO tone: Conviction without arrogance. Calm authority.
- Visuals: Data cards with key metrics, team shots, brand close
- Music: Confident, grounded resolution
- Key rule: If the video is for investors, lead with the metric. If for customers, lead with the human outcome. If for internal, lead with the vision.
- CTA options: Brand tagline, metric cards, strategic vision statement, or emotional customer moment

## Arc 2: The Bookend Arc

**Structure**: Opening Moment → Journey → Return to Opening (Transformed)
**Best for**: Day-in-the-life stories, customer narratives, before/after transformations
**Emotional shape**: Curiosity → Discovery → Recognition → Satisfaction

### Time Splits (120 sec)

| Scene | Time | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| Opening Moment | 0:00–0:15 | Establish a specific, vivid moment |
| The Journey (Beat 1) | 0:15–0:35 | First stage of transformation |
| The Journey (Beat 2) | 0:35–0:60 | Deepening — complexity, stakes rise |
| The Journey (Beat 3) | 0:60–0:80 | Climax or turning point |
| The Return | 0:80–1:05 | Same moment as opening, now transformed |
| Resolution | 1:05–1:20 | Metric/CTA/brand close |

### Scene Descriptions

**Opening Moment**
- Purpose: Drop the audience into a specific, sensory moment (time of day, place, action)
- VO example: "It's 6:45 AM. A phone buzzes — not an alarm..."
- Key rule: Be cinematic. Time, place, action. No abstractions.

**The Journey (2–3 beats)**
- Purpose: Show the transformation unfolding through concrete episodes
- Each beat should escalate — higher stakes, bigger impact, faster pace
- Key rule: Each beat must advance the story. If removing a beat doesn't hurt, cut it.

**The Return**
- Purpose: Circle back to the same person/customer/moment from the opening — but everything has changed
- This is the emotional peak. The audience feels the compression of time, the magnitude of change.
- Key rule: Echo specific language or imagery from the opening. The callback is what makes it land.

**Resolution**
- Metrics, brand close, or strategic statement
- Keep it short — the emotional work is already done

## Arc 3: The Countdown Arc

**Structure**: Stakes → Ticking Clock → Resolution → Payoff
**Best for**: Competitive urgency, crisis response, time-sensitive transformations
**Emotional shape**: Tension → Acceleration → Relief → Triumph

### Time Splits (120 sec)

| Scene | Time | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| The Stakes | 0:00–0:20 | What's at risk — a deal, a customer, a market window |
| The Clock | 0:20–0:30 | Introduce the constraint (deadline, competitor, churn signal) |
| The Sprint (Beat 1) | 0:30–0:55 | First response — speed of action |
| The Sprint (Beat 2) | 0:55–0:80 | Deepening — real-time problem solving |
| Resolution | 0:80–1:00 | The outcome — crisis averted, deal won, customer saved |
| Payoff | 1:00–1:20 | What this means at scale. Metrics, brand close. |

### Scene Descriptions

**The Stakes**
- Open with what could go wrong. A customer threatening to churn. A market window closing. A competitor moving.
- VO tone: Urgent but controlled. Not panicked — professional.

**The Clock**
- Make the constraint tangible: a LinkedIn post, a Slack alert, a deadline on screen
- This scene is short — 10 seconds max. Just enough to feel the pressure.

**The Sprint**
- Show the response happening in real-time. Agents pulling data, prototypes spinning up, demos being customized.
- Visuals should convey speed without feeling chaotic: smooth animations, parallel tracks, split-screens.

**Resolution**
- The customer stays. The deal closes. The feature ships. Show the human reaction.
- Key rule: Let the outcome breathe. Don't rush past the win.

**Payoff**
- Zoom out: this isn't a one-time save. This is the new operating speed. Metrics on screen.

## Arc 4: The Reveal Arc

**Structure**: Mystery/Question → Investigation → Reveal → Implications
**Best for**: Product launches, research findings, surprising data, counter-intuitive insights
**Emotional shape**: Curiosity → Intrigue → Surprise → Understanding

### Time Splits (120 sec)

| Scene | Time | Purpose |
|-------|------|---------|
| The Question | 0:00–0:15 | Pose a compelling question or show a surprising data point |
| The Investigation | 0:15–0:50 | Explore — show the process of discovery |
| The Reveal | 0:50–0:80 | The answer — the product, the insight, the result |
| The Implications | 0:80–1:20 | What this means. Why it matters. What comes next. |

### Scene Descriptions

**The Question**
- Open with a provocative question or a surprising number
- VO example: "What if you could go from customer feedback to board-ready prototype in a single day?"
- Key rule: The question must matter to the audience. If they don't care about the answer, they'll leave.

**The Investigation**
- Show the journey of discovery. Data being analyzed, patterns emerging, prototypes being tested.
- Build intrigue — show partial answers, unexpected findings, moments of "wait, that's interesting..."

**The Reveal**
- The answer lands. Full product demo, the key insight, the transformation moment.
- This is the longest beat. Give it room.

**The Implications**
- So what? What does this mean for the audience, the industry, the market?
- Metrics, forward-looking vision, or a call to action.

## Combining Arcs

Arcs can be combined. The most common hybrid:

**Transformation + Bookend**: Use the 4-scene Transformation structure, but embed a bookend sub-pattern within Scene 3 (e.g., morning customer call → evening follow-up with same customer). This was used in the "Future of Product at Diligent" script.

**Countdown + Reveal**: Open with urgency (countdown), but structure the resolution as a reveal — the audience doesn't see the solution coming.

When combining, one arc is primary (determines scene structure and time splits) and one is secondary (provides a sub-pattern within a scene).
